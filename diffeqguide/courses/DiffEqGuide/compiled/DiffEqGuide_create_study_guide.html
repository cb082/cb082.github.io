Guidelines For Creating A Study Guide

Step 1: First make a list of topics or sections that the assessment will cover. This can start with the big ideas and then you can fill in the finer details. The goal here is to create an outline of the types of questions that can be asked. It should be exhaustive and comprehensive. Many times your textbook will have a summary at the end of each section which has important information about the topics covered in that section. You can use these summaries to create the outline.

Step 2: Make a list of formulas that will be needed for the exam.

Step 3: For any topic that requires a formula, write down that formula or the name of that formula next to the topic.

Step 4: Make a list of terminology that must be known for the exam. Write out in words what each term means.

Step 5: For any topic which has an algorithmic solution write down the step by step algorithm.

Step 6: For each topic, make a list of problems that can be used as practice. These might be examples done in class, homework problems, or problems not assigned from the textbook for which you have the solutions or answers.

Chunking Once your study guide is made, do not try to study everything in one sitting. Instead go through your study guide and schedule times to practice each topic. The most eï¬ƒcient way to do this is to spend more time on things that are your biggest needs. This assumes that these topics will have a significant presence on your assessment. Sometimes there are minor topics which would require a large amount of time to master, but won't make up a large enough portion of the assessment. In these cases, you would want to focus on these topics last and only if time permits.

Practice If you are solving problems with your book open or your notes open, you are not really practicing yet. You want to build up to being able to answer questions without referencing any outside materials. Simply reading solutions to problems is not going to have a high transfer rate.
