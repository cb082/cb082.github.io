Module: Fourier Series (Sines and Cosines)

Learning Objectives

  • Define the Fourier series representation of a periodic function.
  • Compute Fourier coefficients using sine and cosine terms.
  • Use symmetry (even/odd) to simplify calculations.
  • Construct half-range expansions for functions defined on \([0, L]\).
  • Understand convergence properties and the Gibbs phenomenon.

1. Theory and Formulas

Suppose \(f(x)\) is a function of period \(2L\). Its Fourier series expansion is

\[ f(x) \sim \frac{a_0}{2} + \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \left( a_n \cos\!\left(\frac{n\pi x}{L}\right) + b_n \sin\!\left(\frac{n\pi x}{L}\right) \right), \]

with coefficients

\[ a_n = \frac{1}{L}\int_{-L}^{L} f(x)\cos\!\left(\frac{n\pi x}{L}\right)\,dx, \quad b_n = \frac{1}{L}\int_{-L}^{L} f(x)\sin\!\left(\frac{n\pi x}{L}\right)\,dx. \]

and

\[ a_0 = \frac{1}{L}\int_{-L}^{L} f(x)\,dx. \]

Even/Odd Simplifications

  • If \(f\) is even, all \(b_n = 0\) and we use only cosines: \[ a_n = \frac{2}{L}\int_0^L f(x)\cos\!\left(\frac{n\pi x}{L}\right)\,dx. \]
  • If \(f\) is odd, all \(a_n = 0\) and we use only sines: \[ b_n = \frac{2}{L}\int_0^L f(x)\sin\!\left(\frac{n\pi x}{L}\right)\,dx. \]

Half-Range Expansions

If \(f\) is defined only on \([0, L]\):

  • Even extension: Use cosine series only.
  • Odd extension: Use sine series only.

2. Examples

Example 1: \(f(x) = x\) on \([-L, L]\)

Since \(f(x)\) is odd, only sine terms appear:

\[ b_n = \frac{2}{L}\int_0^L x \sin\!\left(\frac{n\pi x}{L}\right)\,dx. \]

Solution Steps Integrate by parts: \[ b_n = \frac{2}{L}\left[-\frac{Lx}{n\pi}\cos\!\left(\frac{n\pi x}{L}\right) \Big|_0^L + \frac{L}{n\pi}\int_0^L \cos\!\left(\frac{n\pi x}{L}\right)dx\right]. \] Simplify and use \(\cos(n\pi) = (-1)^n\). Final result: \[ b_n = \frac{2L}{n\pi}(-1)^{n+1}. \]

Example 2: \(f(x) = x^2\) on \([0, L]\) (even extension)

\[ a_n = \frac{2}{L}\int_0^L x^2\cos\!\left(\frac{n\pi x}{L}\right)\,dx. \]

Hint Use integration by parts twice. Remember that \(\int x^2\cos(kx)\,dx\) yields terms involving \(\sin(kx)\) and \(\cos(kx)\).

3. Practice Problems

  1. Compute the Fourier series of \(f(x) = x\) on \([- \pi, \pi]\).
  2. Compute the Fourier series of \(f(x) = x^2\) on \([- \pi, \pi]\).
  3. Let \(f(x) = \begin{cases}0, & -\pi < x < 0 \\ 1, & 0 \le x < \pi \end{cases}\). Find its Fourier series.
  4. For \(f(x)=x\) on \([0, \pi]\): (a) Find the cosine series (even extension). (b) Find the sine series (odd extension).
  5. Use the Fourier series for \(f(x)=x^2\) on \([- \pi, \pi]\) to show that \[ \sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac{1}{n^2} = \frac{\pi^2}{6}. \]

4. Convergence & Remarks

  • For piecewise-smooth \(f\), the Fourier series converges to \(f(x)\) at points of continuity, and to the midpoint of jumps at discontinuities.
  • Near discontinuities, the series exhibits Gibbs phenomenon (overshoot â‰ˆ 9%).
  • Even/odd symmetry drastically simplifies computation.
